The future of recycling is unstable.

As recycling has blossomed into a global industry, flaws in the system have also grown. Inconsistent recycling standards are confusing to citizens, single-stream recycling leads to high rates of contamination, and Americans continue to generate more and more waste. If we alter the way we think about waste from a burden to a resource, we will reshape our solid waste management industry into a sustainable materials management strategy. Recycling can work if we do, too.

Recycling Is A Success Story

Recycling, composting, and other sustainable waste management strategies are crucial to keep our cities clean. Since the 1960s, increased rates of recycling has been one of the most widespread success stories in American conservation and sustainability.

  • 94% of Americans have access to some type of recycling services and 73% have curbside recycling.
  • Participation is up [Graphic: MSW Recycling and Composting Rates 1960 to 2015 ]
  • In 2017, the U.S. recycling and reuse industry accounted for 470,000 stable jobs paying $30.6 billion in wages. It generated $117 billion in economic activity and $8.2 billion in state, local and federal tax revenues.

What If We Stop Recycling?

Americans generate more waste than any other country in the world. Recent studies indicate that landfills are nearly full, and in some areas, landfills will reach capacity in 15 years.

Landfill capacity will decrease by 15 percent in the next 5 years.

What Can We Do To Fix This Now?

We can create a circular economy, educate consumers, research our current waste, purchase sustainable products and prioritize new infrastructure options and improve our current technology.

Rethinking Current Strategies

The health of our citizens and the safety of our environment depend on sustainable waste management systems. We need to reimagine funding opportunities for recycling programs, create new partnerships with public sector and private sectors working in tandem and pilot programs that are innovative.

The Way Forward

It may seem overwhelming, but start now. Prioritizing and communicating new ways of thinking about the recycling – prevention first, then reduction, reuse, repurpose, recycle/compost, incinerate/waste-to-energy, then landfill as a last resort – helps to create a holistic systems overhaul. Many cities are piloting innovative programs right now that can serve as templates for your own strategy.
